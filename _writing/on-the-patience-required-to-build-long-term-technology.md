The most meaningful systems take years to mature. The semiconductor industry needed four decades before it could power a connected world. Software platforms follow a similar arc—requiring sustained investment before their compounding effects become visible. These are some reflections on why persistence, more than brilliance, determines whether an idea survives.

## The long arc of compounding

We celebrate breakthroughs, but most meaningful technology is the product of sustained, unglamorous effort. The transistor was invented in 1947; it took until the 1990s for it to transform daily life. Between those dates lay decades of patient refinement.

## Against the quarterly mindset

The greatest risk to long-term technology is the short-term incentive structure that surrounds it. When progress is measured in sprints, the systems that require years of gestation are systematically underfunded.

## What endures

The projects that survive are those built by people who measure their work in decades rather than cycles. Persistence is not merely a virtue—it is the mechanism by which compounding effects become visible.
