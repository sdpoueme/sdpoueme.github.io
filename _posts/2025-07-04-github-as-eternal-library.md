I maintained a WordPress site for years. It served its purpose adequately — the interface was familiar, the ecosystem of themes and plugins made customization painless, and the publishing workflow required no technical sophistication beyond what any literate person possesses. But I left it, and the reason is not dissatisfaction with WordPress as software but a deeper unease about the model of preservation it implies.

A WordPress site exists on rented ground. It persists exactly as long as you continue paying for hosting, renewing domains, updating plugins against an ever-shifting landscape of security vulnerabilities. The moment you stop — because you lose interest, because funds are tight, because life intervenes in any of the thousand ways it does — the site decays. First the SSL certificate expires. Then the hosting lapses. Then the domain is released back into the pool, often claimed by squatters who fill your former address with spam. The writing, if you were diligent about backups, survives on a hard drive somewhere. If you were not, it vanishes entirely.

This is a poor model for anything you intend to outlive your attention span.

## The archive as infrastructure

A Git repository operates on different assumptions. The content is distributed — every clone is a complete copy, carrying the full history of every change ever made. GitHub, as a platform, could theoretically disappear, and the repositories would survive in every developer's local copy. More practically, GitHub has demonstrated a credible commitment to long-term preservation through initiatives like the Arctic Code Vault — a deliberate archival gesture that treats repositories as cultural artifacts worth preserving across centuries.

I do not claim that GitHub is eternal in any absolute sense. No institution is. But its architecture — distributed, redundant, designed for collaboration across time — embodies a philosophy of preservation that is fundamentally different from the rental model of conventional web hosting. A repository does not expire when a subscription lapses. It simply waits.

This is the logic of a library, not a storefront. Libraries are built on the assumption that their contents have value beyond the immediate moment of creation — that someone, someday, may want to find what was placed there. A blog on rented hosting is more like a market stall: present only as long as the vendor tends it, gone without trace when they move on.

## Not just code

The decision to host writing on GitHub may seem eccentric — the platform is, after all, designed for source code. But source code and prose share more structural commonalities than is immediately apparent: both are text, both benefit from version history, both gain meaning from context and cross-reference, and both deserve preservation infrastructure that does not degrade with neglect.

What I intend to place here extends beyond technical writing. Progressive ideas that deserve a longer shelf life than a social media post. Genealogies and family histories — documents whose value increases with time rather than diminishing. Biographies of people whose contributions risk being forgotten. Reflections on technology, cities, economics, and culture that I want to exist somewhere more durable than a feed algorithm's mercy.

The topics will range across what interests me: urban intelligence and the data systems that make cities function; the economics and culture of gaming; the African diaspora and its intellectual traditions; questions of infrastructure — both digital and physical — and how it shapes the lives conducted upon it. Not all of it will be polished. Some of it will be provisional, marked as draft, revised over time. Git accommodates this naturally; every commit is a dated assertion, and the history of revisions is itself a kind of intellectual record.

## A deliberate act

Moving to GitHub is not a retreat from accessibility — Jekyll generates static HTML that loads faster and more reliably than any WordPress installation I ever maintained. It is a deliberate choice about what kind of relationship I want with my own writing: not the relationship of a tenant to a landlord, but the relationship of an author to an archive. The writing belongs to me, the preservation infrastructure is designed for permanence, and the cost of maintaining it approaches zero.

That is enough to begin.
